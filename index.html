Mothers won(?) Mexico did not cut children´s classes for the World Cup, but the proposal reignited gender inequality in households

-Around 50% of women take charge of childcare and housework by themselves, the other half do it with help, and 1% of these tasks are done by men alone, in San Luis Potosí.

Source: Pixabay
By Regina Arellano
Long-form data journalism article

When the Public Education System in Mexico (SEP) announced that they would cut children´s classes for over a month for the World Cup, Nancy Hernández, an important Mexican journalist, newsroom coordinator and a mother of three kids, got worried:

“3 months, what am I going to do (with them) for 3 months, how am I going to be able to work?”

In less than a week, the Mexican government retracted its decision after a strong backlash from teachers, parents and the community, who not only criticised the consequences on children´s education, but also on family dynamics and the invisible burden on women.

“Who will take care of the millions of children during this additional month without class? Who will reorganize schedules, jobs, transports and family economies? Who will absorb care jobs that the State decided to translate silently into households? Who will have to ask for permissions, days off at work or refuse certain income? The answer is, one more time, women (…) and no one consulted or listened to them.”

This statement was published by Red de Mujeres Unidas por la Educación, one of the organisations that worried about the consequences this decision would have on women.

Nancy Hernández is one of those women. As a journalist, her daily life consists of a complex task of juggling her work life and motherhood, as millions of women in México.

“There are times when I need to take my kids to the office, because in journalism there are always last-minute calls, a video, an interview or urgent things (…) that I cannot postpone. And if that information emerges in the middle of my activities as a mom, I have to pause this to attend to my job. So, this is when it gets complicated.”

Her life is a constant evaluation of when she can or cannot be replaceable.

“And there are some things with my sons where I cannot be replaced, you know? I try to always evaluate that. If I cannot be replaced in this or that with my son, I look for ways to accommodate myself, and I agree with my husband, so he steps up as well.”

Nancy reflected that, fortunately, she has a support network that helps her to take care of her children when her schedule. Her husband is her principal support, but she also has the help of the children´s grandparents.

“There is a moment when you realise that you cannot do everything by yourself, even if you try hard enough. After all, everyone is expecting you to do it. It is very stigmatised… the belief that mothers can do everything, that they are superheroes. We carry on with that, and we make the effort to comply with that stigma.”

Even if this decision was revoked, and the school calendar will be done as usual, this issue reopened a debate within the gender roles and who holds the most responsibilities in Mexican households.
